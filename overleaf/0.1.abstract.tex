\begin{center}
{\huge \bf Abstract} \
\line(1,0){430}
\end{center}

\vspace{1em}

\noindent Programming of software generates stress and distress for programmers, especially beginners who are under tight time pressures. Although typical programming frameworks give high priority to technical accuracy, they ignore the psychological well-being of the human operator. The current research project presents the design, evaluation, and validation of an unobtrusive machine learning classification tool using software only that runs asynchronously in the operating system layer to classify programmer stress from passive interaction data. This is achieved based on an empirical database of 11,138 unique 30 seconds tumbling windows recorded from 67 computer science undergraduate students undergoing live graded tests. This includes keystroke dynamics at a high level of detail, latency variability when typing on a keyboard, window transitions, rates of text deletions, and compiling time analysis. To counterbalance the extreme 83\% discrepancy in the major class resulting from normal exam pressures, an intense pre-processing approach involving the use of Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) was adopted under a 10×10 Repeated Stratified K-Fold Cross-Validation design. Experimental findings show that a local SVM algorithm employing a Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel can accurately identify coding distress conditions, with a Mean Accuracy score of 69.2\% compared to a random guess accuracy rate of 33.3\%. This provides a scalable, zero-leakage approach to computer science instruction and developer informatics, forming a zero-overhead technical basis for future context-driven adaptive intervention prototypes.
